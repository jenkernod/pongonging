Shadow of the Colossus: The Importance of Gaius and Size Perspective

This purpose of this project is to share the first strong, memorable experience I had when playing a video game. Shadow of The Colossus, orginally a PS2 game from 2005 that has been remastered for both the PS3 and PS4 due to it's popularity. You start the game as this young man, Wander, thrown into a very open world with the hopes of reviving a girl. In order to revive the girl, he must fight and kill 16 colossi across this strange fantasy world. I recommend playing the game as it contains such beautiful imagery and landscaping along with the intense designs of each of the foes which reflects Barthes "The Reality Effect" in The Rustle of Language with emerssive details. Thank You!

In Barthes "The Reality Effect", he states that there is an element of reality that comes apparent from the fine details of game that have nothing to do with plot.I would say this is absolutely correct and Shadow of the Colossus is a wonderful example. There are no English words spoken in the game, similar to LIMBO that we have been studying. You are thrown into a plot that is already taking place. Wander is already starting his mission of reviving the girl. You don't know why, but he wants her to wake up. Your objective is to kill the colossi, revive the girl. Anything else in the game is extra. Throughout the game, you pass these small shrines that typically have a salamander with a glowing tail on them. If you manage to kill, catch, and eat these salamanders, it gives you a minor health/stamina increase. This does not make any difference to the game play or the plot but adds to the enviornment.

Picking up on small things in your enviornment is where you get the feeling of being in an entirely new world. This game is an open world game. Potentially, you can traverse the entire map all at once as opposed to throughout the game if you wish, however, your map function provided in the game the areas are blurred out, blocking the area out so it is not easy to navigate. As you defeat more and more Colossi, more of these clouded map areas unlock and each one has a different landscape. There are some battles that take place underwater, some in the sky, some in grassy plains, etc. There really is not a reason for such variancies other than for the appreciation of the player.

The most memorable of the all of the colossi is the third one you fight, The Knight, AKA Gaius. You must swim onto a bridge ascending to a hovering platform over a lake under dark clouds. When you reach the top of the platform, the large pile of rocks in the center start standing up and form Gaius. This is the first part of the game where you feel particularly small and vulnerable as you can see from Example 1, you see how tall Gaius is compared to boy Wander:
